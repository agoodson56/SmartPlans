Estimator's Review Checklist ESSENTIAL

How to Turn an AI Estimate into a Winning Bid

This section is your practical, step-by-step guide to reviewing and refining the SmartPlans AI-generated estimate before it becomes your final bid. Whether you are a junior estimator building your first proposal or a seasoned veteran looking for a systematic checklist, this guide ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

1. Understanding What SmartPlans Gives You

SmartPlans reads your drawings and specifications using a 27-brain AI engine. It counts devices, estimates cable quantities, prices materials, calculates labor, and generates a professional proposal. That is a tremendous head start, but it is not a replacement for estimator judgment.

Key Mindset: The AI estimate is a strong starting point. Your job is to verify, refine, and finalize. Think of it as a first draft written by a very fast but occasionally imperfect assistant.
What the AI Does WellWhere the AI Needs Your Help
Counts symbols quickly across dozens of sheetsMay miscount devices shown in legends or detail callouts as actual installed devices
Catches buried spec requirements humans often skipCannot judge constructability, site access, or real-world labor conditions
Applies consistent pricing across every line itemMay select the wrong manufacturer or product line if the spec is ambiguous
Generates RFIs from gaps in the drawingsCannot read the general contractor's mind about schedule, phasing, or logistics
Processes addenda and layers in scope changesMay hallucinate quantities when drawings are blurry or cluttered
Bottom Line: Trust the AI for speed and coverage. Trust yourself for accuracy and context. The combination of both is what wins bids.

2. Step-by-Step Review Process

Work through these seven checks in order. Each one builds on the previous, and skipping any of them is where estimators lose money.

A. Verify Device Counts (MOST CRITICAL)

Device counts drive everything downstream: cable quantities, labor hours, and material costs. If the counts are wrong, the entire estimate is wrong.

  1. Open your construction drawings side-by-side with the SmartPlans Bill of Materials (BOM).
  2. Pick 2 to 3 representative floors and hand-count key devices: cameras, card readers, door contacts, pull stations, speakers, WAPs, and data drops.
  3. Compare your hand counts to the AI counts for those same floors. Calculate the percentage difference.
  4. If the AI is within 5%, the counts are likely reliable across the full set. If the difference exceeds 15%, recount the entire drawing set carefully.
Common AI Counting Errors:
  • Counting devices shown in detail drawings or legends as actual installed devices
  • Missing devices on reflected ceiling plans (RCPs) that are separate from the floor plans
  • Double-counting devices shown on both architectural and low-voltage sheets
  • Confusing similar symbols across disciplines (e.g., smoke detector vs. speaker/strobe)
Tip: If the AI count is within 5% of your hand count, it is likely correct across the full drawing set. If it is off by more than 15%, do a full recount. Between 5% and 15%, spot-check additional floors before deciding.

B. Check Cable Quantities

Cable is one of the highest-cost materials on any low-voltage project. A 20% error in cable quantities can swing your bid by tens of thousands of dollars.

CheckWhat to Look ForTypical Range
Average run lengthDivide total cable footage by device count. Does the average make sense for the building size?150 to 250 ft in most commercial buildings
Conduit quantitiesAI often underestimates underground and exterior conduit. Check site plans for trenching runs.Varies widely; verify against site drawings
Backbone cableCompare backbone fiber and copper counts against the riser diagram. Count the number of risers and IDF-to-MDF connections.Should match riser diagram exactly
Waste factorEnsure a waste factor is included. Standard is 10% for cable, 15% for conduit fittings.1.10x multiplier on cable; 1.15x on fittings
Quick Cable Sanity Check: Total cable = Device count x Average run length x 1.1 (waste). If the AI total is more than 15% different from this formula, investigate the individual runs.

C. Review Material Specifications

The spec book is the law of the project. If the AI priced the wrong product line, you will either overbid (and lose the job) or underbid (and lose money).

  • Manufacturer match — Verify the AI selected the correct manufacturer. If the spec says Axis cameras but the AI priced Hanwha, correct it before generating your proposal.
  • Approved equals — Check whether the spec allows "approved equal" substitutions or is locked to a specific brand. If substitutions are allowed, you may be able to use lower-cost products.
  • Cable types — Confirm plenum vs. riser rated, Cat6 vs. Cat6A, and fiber strand counts match the spec requirements exactly.
  • OFCI items — Look for spec sections that designate equipment as Owner-Furnished, Contractor-Installed (OFCI). These items should be priced at $0 for material but still carry labor hours for installation.
Note: Use the Editable BOM feature to correct any manufacturer or pricing errors directly in the table. Changes recalculate totals in real time.

Add 25% to 50% for high ceilings (over 15 ft), occupied spaces, secure facilities, or confined areas. Union projects may also have different productivity rates and crew composition requirements.

F. Review Pricing

The AI Grand Total represents the raw estimated cost based on the selected pricing tier and regional multiplier. Your final bid price will layer additional costs on top.

  • Rate Library — Apply your company's known pricing from past projects. This overrides AI estimates with your actual historical costs.
  • High-dollar items — Individually verify pricing on servers, NVRs, head-end equipment, fire alarm control panels, and generators. A $2,000 error on a single head-end unit changes your bid significantly.
  • Regional accuracy — Confirm the regional pricing multiplier is appropriate. Sacramento pricing is not the same as San Francisco pricing. Adjust the multiplier in project setup if needed.
  • Benchmark feature — If you have actuals from past projects recorded in SmartPlans, use the Benchmark feature to compare AI pricing against your real-world costs.
Important: The AI Grand Total is the raw material + labor cost. Your proposal price adds labor markup, material markup, overhead, burden, profit, and contingency on top. Make sure you understand the difference before quoting a number to a GC.

G. Finalize Exclusions & Assumptions

Exclusions and assumptions are your legal protection. A missing exclusion can cost you the entire profit margin on a project. This is not optional.

  1. Review the auto-generated exclusions that SmartPlans created from the AI analysis. Remove any that do not apply to your project.
  2. Add project-specific exclusions. Common examples: "Excludes work above 30 ft requiring scaffolding or lifts," "Assumes normal business hours access (7AM to 5PM)," "Excludes permit fees unless noted."
  3. Verify your assumptions match reality. If you assumed open ceilings but the building has hard-lid ceilings, your labor estimate is wrong. Call the GC or owner's rep to clarify unknowns.
  4. Review clarifications to ensure scope boundaries are crystal clear. Ambiguity in scope is where disputes and change orders originate.
Tip: Use the Exclusions & Assumptions Builder and load the discipline-specific templates as a starting point. Then customize for your project. It is far better to have too many exclusions than too few.

3. Common Pitfalls That Lose Money

These are the top ten mistakes that cost estimators real dollars. Every one of them has happened on actual projects. Learn from the losses of others.

#PitfallHow to Avoid It
1Not reading the spec addendaUpload all addenda to SmartPlans. The AI reads them, but you should too. Addenda frequently change quantities, products, or scope.
2Missing liquidated damages clausesRead the general conditions carefully. LD clauses can be $500 to $5,000 per day. Factor schedule risk into your contingency.
3Underestimating travel and per diemUse the SmartPlans Travel & Per Diem calculator. Account for crew size, multiple mobilizations, and realistic hotel rates.
4Forgetting permit fees and inspectionsContact the local AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction) for permit costs. Budget for re-inspection fees as well.
5Not accounting for phased constructionWorking around occupied spaces is 25% to 50% slower. Use Multi-Phase Pricing to structure phased bids properly.
6Missing prevailing wage requirementsCheck for Davis-Bacon (federal) or state prevailing wage. SmartPlans supports both; set the correct wage type in project setup.
7Underestimating conduit and pathwayConduit is the most frequently under-bid item in low-voltage work. Walk the site if possible. Add 15% to 20% contingency on pathway.
8Not including commissioning and testing laborBudget 8% to 12% of total labor hours for system testing, commissioning, and owner training.
9Forgetting warranty period costsMost contracts require 1 to 2 years of warranty. Budget for service calls, replacement parts, and return trips.
10Not reading the general conditionsWho provides temporary power? Dumpsters? Material hoisting? If the GC does not, the cost falls on you.

Getting Started

What Is SmartPlans?

SmartPlans is a website that reads your construction drawings for you. You upload your blueprints and spec books as PDF files. The AI looks at every page, counts all the devices, figures out how much cable you need, prices everything out, and gives you a complete bid. It does in minutes what used to take days by hand.

What Do You Need?

  • Your construction drawings saved as a PDF file.
  • Your specification book (also called "specs") saved as a PDF file.
  • A web browser like Chrome, Edge, or Firefox.
  • An internet connection.
Tip: PDF files work the best. The AI can read both the pictures and the text inside a PDF.

Open SmartPlans

Go to https://smartplans-4g5.pages.dev/ in your web browser. You will see 7 steps across the top of the page. Each step is a button you click when you are ready to move forward. Just go from Step 1 to Step 7, one at a time.

Good to know: You do not need to install anything. SmartPlans runs right in your browser.

Step 1 — Tell Us About Your Project

What Is This?

Step 1 is the single most important page in SmartPlans. Every field you fill in here controls how the AI reads your drawings, what prices it uses, how it calculates labor, and what appears on your final proposal. Getting Step 1 right means your estimate is built on the correct foundation. Getting it wrong means every number downstream is off.

This page is divided into four major sections: Project Information, Prevailing Wage / Davis-Bacon, Travel & Per Diem, and Pricing & Rate Configuration. Every field is explained below in the order it appears on screen.

Do not skip fields on this page. SmartPlans uses these inputs to drive material pricing, labor costing, regional adjustments, wage compliance, and travel budgets. A blank field does not mean zero — it means the AI will use a default, and that default may not match your project.

Section: Project Information

1. Project Name Required

A free-text name for this bid. This name appears on the proposal cover page, in the exported BOM, in the SmartPM project schedule, and in your Saved Estimates list. Use something descriptive enough that you can find it six months from now.

Example: Sunrise Medical Center Phase 2

2. Prepared For Optional

The name of the general contractor, government agency, or end-client who will receive this bid. This value is printed on the proposal cover page. If you leave it blank, the cover page will omit the "Prepared For" line.

6. Specific Items to Count Optional

A free-text field where you tell the AI exactly what to look for on the plans. The more specific you are, the more accurate the count. If you leave this blank, the AI will use its own judgment based on the symbol legend and drawing content.

Good examples:

  • Count all 4MP dome cameras and 8MP bullet cameras separately
  • Look for wireless access points (WAPs) shown as triangles with "W"
  • Count pull stations and horn/strobes on fire alarm sheets only
Tip: This field is especially useful when drawings use non-standard symbols or when you need the AI to distinguish between similar devices (e.g., indoor vs. outdoor cameras).

7. Known Quantities Optional

If you have already hand-counted some items from the plans, enter them here. SmartPlans uses your known quantities as a verification benchmark. After the AI completes its count, you can compare the AI totals against your known quantities to gauge accuracy.

Example: 48 cameras, 12 card readers, 6 pull stations

How it works: Your known quantities do not override the AI count. They appear side-by-side in the review step so you can spot discrepancies. If the AI says 52 cameras and you hand-counted 48, you know to look more closely.

8. Building Code Jurisdiction Optional

The building code edition that governs this project. The AI uses this to check code-related requirements like maximum travel distance to pull stations, notification appliance spacing, and ADA mounting heights.

Examples: IBC 2021, California CBC 2022, NFPA 72 2022

9. Project Location Required

The city and state where the project is located. SmartPlans uses this for two critical calculations:

  • Regional pricing — Material and labor costs vary dramatically by geography. A camera install in San Francisco costs significantly more than the same install in rural Oklahoma.
  • Travel calculation — If you enable the Travel section, the project location is the destination for mileage and airfare calculations.
Do not skip the location. If you leave it blank, SmartPlans uses national average pricing, which may be 20-40% off from your actual local costs.

Section: Prevailing Wage / Davis-Bacon

This section controls whether your estimate uses prevailing wage labor rates. Prevailing wage is required on public works projects (schools, government buildings, hospitals) and on any project receiving federal funding. If you are bidding private commercial work, set this to "None."

Labor Classifications

Once you select a county or metro area, SmartPlans displays the loaded rate for each classification. Here is what each person does on the jobsite:

ClassificationRole on the Jobsite

Blended Crew Rate

SmartPlans calculates a Blended Crew Rate that represents the average hourly cost of your field crew based on a realistic crew mix:

ClassificationCrew Mix %Why This Percentage
Comm Installer (Journeyman)60%Most of the crew are journeymen doing the bulk of the installation work
Comm Technician (Lead)25%One lead tech per crew handles terminations, testing, and supervision
Foreman10%One foreman may cover multiple crews or split time between projects
Apprentice5%Apprentice ratio is limited by union rules and apprenticeship programs

Formula: Blended Rate = (Journeyman × 0.60) + (Lead × 0.25) + (Foreman × 0.10) + (Apprentice × 0.05)

Why blended? Using a single blended rate simplifies estimating. Instead of assigning individual classifications to each task, SmartPlans applies the blended rate to all field labor hours. This averages out to be very close to reality across a full project.

Section: Travel & Per Diem

This section is for out-of-town projects where your crew needs to travel to the jobsite. The travel cost is calculated as a separate line item on your bid — it is not mixed into labor or materials. This makes it easy for the GC to see exactly what travel costs, and it is standard practice on government and prevailing wage work.

12. Include Travel Costs Optional

A checkbox that enables the entire Travel section. When unchecked, all travel fields are hidden and no travel cost is added to the bid. Check this box if your crew will need to drive or fly to the project and stay overnight.

13. Crew Size

The number of technicians who will travel to the jobsite. This multiplies hotel and per diem costs. Do not include the project manager here unless they will be on-site and staying overnight.

Example: A crew of 4 technicians staying 5 nights at $150/night = 4 × 5 × $150 = $3,000 in hotel costs per trip.

14. Number of Trips

How many separate mobilizations to the jobsite. Most projects have at least two trips:

  • Trip 1 — Rough-In: Pull cable, install backboards, mount boxes, run pathway
  • Trip 2 — Trim-Out: Mount devices, terminate, test, and commission

Large projects may have 3-5 trips. Each trip is a separate round of hotel, per diem, and travel costs.

15. Days Per Trip

The number of working days on-site per trip. This is calendar working days, not including travel days. If you drive on Sunday and start work Monday through Friday, that is 5 days. SmartPlans uses this to calculate hotel nights and per diem days.

16. Hotel $/Night

The nightly hotel rate per room. SmartPlans assumes one room per person (prevailing wage projects typically require single occupancy).

What is the GSA rate? GSA stands for General Services Administration. The federal government publishes maximum hotel rates for every city in the country. These "GSA rates" are the standard benchmark for government-funded projects. If your project is Davis-Bacon, use the GSA rate for that city. You can look up GSA rates at gsa.gov/travel/plan-book/per-diem-rates. Rates range from $98/night in rural areas to $350+/night in major cities.

17. Per Diem $/Day

The daily meals and incidentals allowance per person per day. Per diem covers:

  • Breakfast — typically $15-20
  • Lunch — typically $15-20
  • Dinner — typically $30-40
  • Incidentals — tips, laundry, personal phone calls, parking meters

The GSA standard M&IE (Meals & Incidentals) rate is $79/day for most California counties. Higher-cost cities like San Francisco and Los Angeles have rates of $79-$84/day. Check the GSA per diem lookup for your project city.

18. Mileage (Round Trip Miles)

The total round-trip driving distance in miles from your office to the jobsite. SmartPlans multiplies this by the IRS standard mileage rate ($0.70/mile for 2024) to calculate mileage reimbursement.

Mileage OR Airfare — not both. Use mileage for projects you will drive to (typically under 500 miles). Use airfare for projects 500+ miles away. Do not enter both.

19. Airfare $/Person

Round-trip economy airfare per person. Use this for projects that are too far to drive (roughly 500+ miles or any project requiring a flight). Check current airfare prices on Google Flights or your corporate travel tool.

20. Rental Car $/Day

Daily rental car rate. Your crew will need a vehicle at the jobsite, typically a truck or SUV that can carry tools, ladders, and materials. Enterprise/National corporate rates for a full-size truck run $65-120/day depending on the city.

21. Parking $/Day

Daily parking cost at the jobsite or hotel. Urban jobsites (downtown hospitals, high-rises) can run $25-50/day for parking. Suburban and rural sites may have free parking. Do not forget hotel parking — some city hotels charge $30-40/night.

Travel Cost Calculation

SmartPlans calculates total travel cost using these formulas:

How it works: SmartPlans multiplies every raw material price by this value. If a Cat6A cable box costs $320 at the national average and you select West Coast (1.25), SmartPlans prices it at $320 × 1.25 = $400.

24. Labor Rates

25. Include Labor Burden Important

A checkbox with an adjustable percentage (default: 35%). Labor burden is the additional cost on top of a worker's base hourly wage that covers taxes, insurance, and benefits. Every employer pays these costs — they are real and must be included in your bid.

Components of labor burden:

ComponentTypical %What It Is
FICA7.65%Social Security (6.2%) + Medicare (1.45%) employer match. Every employer pays this — it is federal law.
FUTA / SUTA2 – 6%Federal and state unemployment insurance. FUTA is 0.6% (after credit). SUTA varies by state and your company's experience rating.
Workers Compensation5 – 12%Injury insurance. Low-voltage work is typically classification code 5537. Rates vary by state and your company's loss history (mod rate).
General Liability3 – 5%Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance allocated per labor dollar. Your CGL policy premium divided across all labor.
Health & Welfare8 – 15%Medical, dental, and vision insurance for employees. Union projects have fixed H&W contributions per hour worked.
Pension / 401k0 – 6%Retirement contributions. Union projects have fixed pension contributions. Non-union may have 401k match.

Formula:

Loaded Rate = Base Hourly Rate × (1 + Burden %)

Example: A journeyman at $68.90/hr with 35% burden: $68.90 × 1.35 = $93.02/hr loaded

Do not skip burden. If you bid at bare labor rates without burden, you are losing 35% on every labor dollar. On a $200,000 labor bid, that is $70,000 in unrecovered costs. Burden is not profit — it is real expense your company pays on every paycheck.

26. Markup Percentages

Four separate markup categories. Markup is applied after all costs are calculated. This is how your company makes profit and covers overhead (rent, trucks, office staff, insurance, tools).

Markup TypeDefaultApplied ToExample
Material Markup50%All material costs (after regional multiplier)$100 cost → $150 sell price
Labor Markup50%All labor costs (after burden is applied)$100 loaded labor → $150 sell price
Equipment Markup15%Rental equipment: lifts, cable pullers, fusion splicers$1,000 rental → $1,150 sell price
Subcontractor Markup10%Work performed by subcontractors (e.g., fire alarm sub, electrician)$50,000 sub cost → $55,000 sell price
Why different markups? Materials carry higher markup because you handle procurement, warehousing, delivery, and warranty. Equipment has lower markup because there is less risk. Subcontractor markup is lowest because the sub carries their own overhead and insurance — you are just managing them.
Tip: Industry standard for low-voltage contractors is 40-60% on materials and labor. If you are bidding competitively against larger firms, 40-45% may win more work. If you are the only qualified bidder, 55-60% is reasonable. Adjust based on your market.

Section: How the Bid Price Is Calculated

This is the complete formula SmartPlans uses to get from AI-counted quantities to your final bid number. Understanding this formula lets you reverse-engineer any line item and explain your pricing to a GC.

  1. Raw Material Cost
    Raw Materials = AI Quantities × Tier Unit Prices × Regional Multiplier
    The AI counts every device on the plans. Each device is priced at Budget, Mid, or Premium tier. The regional multiplier adjusts for local market conditions.
  2. Material Sell Price
    Material Sell = Raw Materials × (1 + Material Markup %)
    Your material markup (default 50%) is applied to the raw cost. This covers procurement, handling, and profit.
  3. Labor Cost (Loaded)
    Labor Cost = AI Labor Hours × Hourly Rate × (1 + Burden %)
    The AI estimates hours for each task (cable pull, device mount, termination, testing). Hours are multiplied by the blended rate, then burden is applied.
  4. Labor Sell Price
    Labor Sell = Loaded Labor × (1 + Labor Markup %)
    Your labor markup (default 50%) is applied to the fully loaded labor cost.
  5. Equipment Sell Price
    Equipment Sell = Equipment Costs × (1 + Equipment Markup %)
    Rental equipment (lifts, testers, pullers) with your equipment markup (default 15%).
  6. Subcontractor Sell Price
    Sub Sell = Subcontractor Costs × (1 + Sub Markup %)
    Any subcontracted work with your sub markup (default 10%).
  7. Subtotal
    Subtotal = Material Sell + Labor Sell + Equipment Sell + Sub Sell + Travel
    All sell prices plus the travel line item (if enabled) are added together.
  8. Contingency
    Contingency = Subtotal × 10%
    A 10% contingency to cover unknowns: change orders, scope creep, material waste, rework, and unforeseen conditions.
  9. Grand Total
    GRAND TOTAL = Subtotal + Contingency
    This is your final bid number — the price on the proposal cover page.
Sanity Check: After SmartPlans generates your estimate, check the ratio of materials to labor. For most low-voltage projects, materials should be 40-55% and labor should be 45-60% of the pre-markup total. If materials are 80% and labor is 20%, something is wrong — either the AI overcounted devices or undercounted labor hours. Go back to Step 6 and review.
Tip: You can adjust any of these percentages on Step 1 and SmartPlans will instantly recalculate the entire estimate. Use this to run "what-if" scenarios: What happens if I drop markup to 40%? What if burden goes up to 42%? What if I use Budget tier instead of Mid? This is how experienced estimators dial in a competitive number.

Step 2 — Upload Your Symbol Legend

What Is a Symbol Legend?

A symbol legend is a special page in your drawings. It shows a list of little symbols (like circles, squares, and triangles) and tells you what each one means. For example, a small circle might mean "camera" and a triangle might mean "speaker."

How to Use It

  1. Find the legend page in your drawing set. It is usually one of the first pages.
  2. Click the upload area on this step. A file picker will open.
  3. Pick the PDF page or pages that show the symbol legend.
  4. You will see a preview of what you uploaded. Make sure it looks right.
Why does this matter? The AI uses this legend to understand what all the symbols on your floor plans mean. Without it, the AI has to guess — and guessing is less accurate.
Tip: If your drawings do not have a separate legend page, that is okay. The AI will try to figure out the symbols on its own. But uploading a legend makes it much more accurate.

Step 3 — Upload Your Floor Plans

What Are Floor Plans?

Floor plans are the drawings that show where all the devices go. They show the layout of the building and all the little symbols for cameras, data drops, speakers, and more.

How to Use It

  1. Click the upload area or drag your PDF file into it.
  2. Upload the full drawing set as one PDF file.
  3. SmartPlans will split each page apart automatically. You will see a thumbnail preview of each page.
  4. If any pages are not floor plans (like a title page or a table of contents), you can remove them.
Big files are okay! If your PDF is very large, SmartPlans will split it into smaller chunks automatically. You do not need to do anything special.
Tip: Only upload pages that have actual device symbols on them. Remove title pages, general notes, and cover sheets for the best results.

Step 4 — Upload Your Specifications

What Are Specifications?

Specifications (or "specs") are the written document that comes with the drawings. The specs tell the contractor exactly what products to use and how to install them. For example, the specs might say "Use Axis brand cameras" or "All cables must be plenum-rated."

How to Use It

  1. Click the upload area on this step.
  2. Pick the spec book PDF from your computer.
  3. SmartPlans will read through all the pages and find the important requirements.
Tip: If there are addenda (changes to the original specs), you can upload those too in the next step. Addenda often change products or quantities, so they are very important.

Step 5 — Review RFIs

What Is an RFI?

RFI stands for "Request for Information." An RFI is a question you ask the architect or engineer about something that is not clear in the drawings or specs. For example: "The drawings show a camera at the front door, but the specs do not list a camera model. Which camera should we use?"

How to Use It

  1. SmartPlans looks at your drawings and specs and finds things that are unclear or missing.
  2. It creates a list of suggested RFIs for you.
  3. Each RFI has a checkbox next to it.
  4. Check the ones you want to include in your bid. Uncheck the ones you do not need.
  5. The checked RFIs will be added to your proposal.
Why include RFIs? Including RFIs in your proposal shows the general contractor that you did your homework. You read the drawings carefully and found the gaps. This makes your bid look more professional.

Step 6 — Your Estimate Is Ready

This is the big page where all your results show up. It has a lot of sections, so let us walk through each one.

The Big Number at the Top

What Is This?

This is your total bid price. It includes all your materials, labor, markups, overhead, profit, and contingency. This is the number you would put on your bid form.

Important: Always double-check this number against your BOM table below. Make sure everything adds up the way you expect before you send this to anyone.

BOM Table (Bill of Materials)

What Is This?

BOM stands for "Bill of Materials." This is a big table that lists every single item in your estimate. It shows the item name, how many you need, the cost per item, and the total cost for that item.

How to Use It

  1. Look through the table to see all the items.
  2. If any number looks wrong, click on it. You can type in a new number.
  3. When you change a number, the totals update right away. You do not need to press a save button.
Tip: Click on any quantity or price to edit it. This is the fastest way to fix mistakes the AI made.

Exclusions & Assumptions

What Is This?

Exclusions are things that are NOT included in your bid. For example: "We are not including any work above 30 feet." Assumptions are things you are assuming to be true. For example: "We assume the ceilings are accessible." These protect you legally if something goes wrong.

How to Use It

  1. Click "Load Defaults" to start with a standard list of exclusions that most projects need.
  2. Click "Auto-Generate" to let the AI create exclusions based on what it found in your drawings.
  3. Read through the list. Add anything that is missing. Remove anything that does not apply.
Do not skip this! Exclusions are your legal protection. If you forget to exclude something, you might have to do that work for free.

Bid Strategy

What Is This?

Bid Strategy lets you set different markup percentages for different categories of work. You can also set your confidence level for each category.

How to Use It

  1. Look at each category (like "Cameras" or "Cabling").
  2. Set a markup percentage for materials and labor in each category.
  3. Set a confidence level: High, Medium, or Low.
    • High confidence = You trust the numbers. Lower contingency is added.
    • Low confidence = You are not sure about the numbers. Higher contingency is added to protect you.
  4. Click "Apply Strategy" to update your totals.

Bid Phases

What Is This?

Sometimes a project is split into parts. The "Base Bid" is the main part. Then there are "Alternates" — extra things the client might want to add or remove. Bid Phases lets you organize your estimate this way.

How to Use It

  1. Your main estimate starts as the Base Bid.
  2. You can move items into an Add Alternate (extra work that costs more).
  3. You can also create a Deduct Alternate (work removed to save money).
  4. The proposal will show a summary table of all phases.
Example: Base Bid = all cameras and cabling. Add Alternate 1 = upgrade to 4K cameras. Deduct Alternate 1 = remove the parking lot cameras.

Competitor Bid Comparison

What Is This?

If you have another company's bid (as an Excel or CSV file), you can upload it here. SmartPlans will compare their prices to yours, item by item.

How to Use It

  1. Type the competitor's name in the name box.
  2. Click "Upload Bid" and pick their Excel or CSV file.
  3. You will see a comparison with green rows (you are cheaper) and red rows (they are cheaper).
  4. Use this to decide where you can sharpen your prices.
  5. Click "Clear Comparison" when you are done.
Important: The competitor's bid data is NOT saved. Once you refresh the page, it is gone. Take a screenshot if you need to keep it.

Rate Library

What Is This?

The Rate Library is your personal price database. It saves the prices from your past projects so you can use them again on future projects. Think of it like a notebook where you write down how much things cost — except the computer remembers it for you.

How to Use It

  1. After you finish an estimate, click "Save Rates from This Estimate." This saves all the current prices to your library.

Actuals & Benchmarks

What Is This?

After a project is finished and all the bills are paid, you can go back and tell SmartPlans what you actually spent. Then, on future projects, SmartPlans can show you "Based on 3 past projects, this item usually costs about $X." This helps you make better estimates over time.

How to Use It

  1. Go to your Saved Estimates (click "Saved" in the header).
  2. Find the project that is finished. Click "Record Actuals."
  3. Enter what you actually spent on materials and labor for each category.
  4. SmartPlans compares your estimate to the real numbers and shows the difference.
  5. Next time you estimate a similar project, SmartPlans shows you benchmarks from your real data.
How benchmarks work: The more projects you record, the better the benchmarks get. After a few projects, you will see messages like "Based on 3 past projects, this item averages $X."
Important: This only works if you go back and enter the real numbers after the project is done. Make it a habit!

Travel & Per Diem

What Is This?

If the project is in another city and your crew has to travel, you need to add travel costs to your bid. This section calculates hotel, meals, gas, airfare, rental cars, and parking.

How to Use It

  1. On Step 1, look for the "Travel & Per Diem" section and check "Include travel costs in estimate."
  2. Enter your crew size — how many people are traveling.
  3. Enter the number of trips. Most projects need 1 to 3 trips.
  4. Enter days per trip — how many days each trip lasts.
  5. Fill in the costs:
    • Hotel rate — How much per night, per person.
    • Per diem — Daily meal money per person. (Per diem means "per day" in Latin.)
    • Mileage — Round trip driving distance. Use this if driving.
    • Airfare — Round trip flight cost per person. Use this if flying.
    • Rental car — Daily rate for a vehicle at the job site.
    • Parking — Daily parking cost.
  6. The total travel cost gets added to your bid automatically.
Tip: Use mileage OR airfare, not both. If you are driving, enter mileage and set airfare to $0. If you are flying, enter airfare and set mileage to 0.
